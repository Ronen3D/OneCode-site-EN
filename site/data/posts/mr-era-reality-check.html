By: Tamar Konig, Product & Project Manager, OneCode

Now is the time for forward-thinking app developers and entrepreneurs to look beyond the smartphone screen, into the physical-virtual space around them, and to be first movers in the MR revolution.

In the mid-1990s we experienced the mobile revolution. Less than 15 years later, we went through the smartphone revolution — which will probably not last much longer than that. The reason is that over the next two years we will undergo yet another revolution: the MR — Mixed Reality — revolution, whose early signs are visible today in what we call VR and AR. MR is expected to create experiences that blend digital information delivered to us either by entering a fully virtual 3D world — VR — or by overlaying an information layer onto the existing world — AR.

Today, VR is used mainly for games, while AR is used beyond games for adding an information layer on top of the world. And yet, we are already beginning to see app developments that take VR out of the gaming world to create virtual conference rooms or deliver a new virtual mall shopping experience.

Is MR Already Here?

At its most recent launch event, Apple provided an impressive demonstration of AR capabilities: a collaboration with IKEA to display furniture in your home — including matching the furniture's size and lighting — a robot battle game against friends on a table, and displaying stats on the field while watching a baseball game. Apple's capability showcase comes several months after it launched its AR development engine, which allows app developers to easily incorporate augmented reality features.

Facebook isn't sitting on the sidelines either. It clearly signaled the direction it was heading with the acquisition of Oculus back in 2014, and it has no intention of stepping back.

The expertise accumulated at OneCode and our specialization in 3D and rich client applications allows us to tailor our work to each client's and project's requirements, including the use of dedicated components that maximize the user experience — even in 3D.

The MR revolution will hinge on expected and necessary advances in two core technologies: first, fitting everything our smartphones can do into the form factor of a regular pair of glasses; and second, creating a high-quality 3D scanner capable of translating even small finger movements in mid-air into user gestures.

One example of such technology is the ability to detect the pressure of a tap on a surface (like a table). Apple is leading this effort — according to reports, roughly 600 engineers are currently working on this topic at the company. Of course, a similar number of engineers are likely working on the same things at Google, Microsoft, Facebook, and numerous startups.

The Hardware Challenge: Hands-Free from the Smartphone

The expectation is that within two to five years, we will all be wearing glasses instead of holding a smartphone in our hands. These glasses will run 2D apps and holograms projected into space, along with games that take us into an entirely different world — or simply add another layer of information on top of the existing one.

The glasses that look closest to the real thing today are Microsoft's HoloLens. But they are still bulky, too expensive, and have a small field of view — all factors that keep them from being accessible to everyone.

VR + AR Software Development

Alongside the expected hardware advances, there is also an expectation of progress on the software side. The development of sophisticated 3D worlds is set to be a key driver of success for virtual worlds. We got a taste of VR and AR's potential with the Pokémon Go app, but we also understood that there is still a long way to go. In the end, that app presented only very partial 3D and limited interaction with the environment. The 3D capabilities of the next generation of virtual worlds will need to run smoothly on a mobile device with the relatively weak processing power of a wearable — such as glasses.

Today's heavyweight engines, designed primarily for gaming, won't be up to the task.

At OneCode, we already tackle today's demands for 3D development for AR systems — primarily applications that implement augmented reality in the fields of entertainment, consumer goods, medicine, and more — none of which can rely on the powerful computing systems found in personal computers. The solution typically lies in using WebGL and Unity3D even for basic graphics needs. As these environments advance, AR/VR development will also accelerate rapidly.

In Conclusion…

If there are still skeptical voices about wearing glasses for augmented reality, you only need to recall the first mobile phones in the early 1990s that looked like a brick. We are now in a similar place with glasses — only things are advancing much faster. As far-fetched as it sounds, just as we have already gotten used to seeing people talking to themselves in the street, in the coming years we will start seeing people waving their hands as they operate virtual apps displayed in the space in front of them.

The expectation is that 2018 will be the breakthrough year, but most people wearing glasses will probably not be seen until the end of the current decade or the start of the next. In any case, anyone developing a new app needs to take these developments into account and prepare for what is coming.
