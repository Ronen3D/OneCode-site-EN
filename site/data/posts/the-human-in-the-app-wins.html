By: Tamar Konig, Product & Project Manager, OneCode

Lately, we have been seeing more entrepreneurs and organizations that are not tech-native by DNA who understand they need to make the leap to the digital world. For me, this is a very different experience from the "typical" entrepreneur (if such a thing exists) who usually comes to us with an app idea and is looking for the best way to bring it to life. The entrepreneurs I'm talking about generally come from an existing organization or business, and they are looking for ways to digitize it or to embed the advantages of digital technology into their existing operations. Both for us, as a software house, and for the entrepreneur, this kind of process requires a different approach and different processes than what we're accustomed to. I want to share one such process we went through with Rami Gertler, an educator and entrepreneur at heart, who came to us wanting to digitize his existing business operations.

As happens in many good cases, our paths crossed somewhat by chance. One of OneCode's employees sought out Rami Gertler's expertise to diagnose English-language learning difficulties in his child. One thing led to another, and it turned out that Rami had been planning for some time to convert to a digital format the didactic diagnostic tests he had developed to identify learning disabilities in English (among native Hebrew speakers). A conversation about the challenges involved led to a meeting, and we set out on the road for a system specification project.

By the end of the process I found that it was a "educational" case study for me — both because of the field the project dealt with, and because we were working with an entrepreneur who had a keen diagnostic eye for learning processes, not just in the language domain but in the development world as well. The following are the key takeaways we gathered:

1. Building a Linguistic Bridge Between the Entrepreneur and the Technology and the Developer

We quickly discovered a gap in the dialogue between us — the development team — and the entrepreneur. We saw that not every concept we referenced was understood in exactly the way we intended. This also manifested in the specification document, which was not always clear enough to someone who doesn't come from the tech world.

Rami Gertler was quick to identify this language gap — which is, after all, his area of expertise. It was interesting to discover that language learning disabilities can also affect fields that have nothing to do with language. Whether it's learning Chinese, learning to code, international relations, or psychology, each has its own specialized vocabulary to master. To use an example from Rami's area of work: when an ordinary person talks about "trauma," they're referring to an unpleasant experience or a nightmare they went through, but in medicine, "trauma" is a type of injury — an event that affects the course of a life going forward.

Therefore, as a lesson from this project, we concluded that there is a need to align communication and language between the entrepreneur and the development team. For example, it's possible to produce a more concise spec that is written in a more user-friendly or graphical way.

2. Keeping the Spec Focused

A focused spec allows for a deeper examination of the venture — for example, to fully understand the investment required. A focused spec also makes it possible to keep it short and clear for an entrepreneur who is not used to documents of this type. By contrast, if you choose to create an overly broad spec, it's easy to get lost in a sea of considerations and options. This is true for tech entrepreneurs; it's even truer in our case — Rami's diagnostic system has more than 20 subtests, and we chose just one of them. The focused spec also allowed us to build it as a platform that would support future developments and ideas (see the New Business Opportunities section below).

3. The Interface – Finding the Balance Between Experience and Functionality

There are very clear trends in UI/UX that are typically followed in a standard development process — and only occasionally do we try to break through with entirely new interfaces. But when working with more established, mature professional disciplines — such as education and diagnostics, or medicine — it is necessary to factor in the professional requirements and give them special consideration. In our case, we were required to find where the line falls between the need to adhere to a didactic/research structure and the need for a user-friendly interface.

One related point that came up during our spec process was the need to build a learning curve for using the app, tailored to the project — in the case of an educational app, you need to think carefully about who the user is and how to turn them into a "fluent speaker of the interface language."

In regular apps, most users who activate them are people who sought them out proactively, and they have some degree of technical background. In such apps, you can also allow room for error — as developers, we're willing to accept users who don't understand how to use them and drop off. In education, by contrast, some users don't come from a technical background, and they are sometimes required to use the app out of obligation rather than choice. If a user doesn't understand how to use it, that will interfere with the didactic process and skew the diagnostic results. Therefore, we must ensure that all users understand exactly what their task is and how to complete it. This doesn't mean we give up on advanced interfaces, but we do need to ensure their correct use — so in the spec we built, 6 screens were dedicated to learning how to use the app, compared to one or two in other apps.

4. Identifying New Business Opportunities

An important part of the spec process for existing businesses is to try to identify opportunities that weren't thought of at all. Very often the original goal — as in our case — will be a simple digitization of an existing process. But by understanding the broader business activity, you can arrive at far-reaching conclusions. During our analysis process, the possibility arose of adapting the tests for a broader user base beyond professional diagnosticians — such as a condensed test adapted for personal use or by teachers, or the possibility of global expansion. A spec grounded in a deep understanding of the business, supported by the development team's business experience, makes it possible to identify these opportunities and adapt the spec accordingly.

5. Advanced Applications

Similarly to identifying business opportunities, you should also examine technological opportunities that weren't originally considered. For example, evaluating AR and 3D interfaces, or even exploring the advantages of virtual reality and the use of AI to create entirely new value for users and/or the venture.

At the end of the process we produced a very focused spec for a single selected subtest, capable of supporting scalability to additional tests, while also enabling the exploitation of future business and technological opportunities. The end result we reached would not have been possible without "the human in the app" — research into the business environment, collaborative work with the entrepreneur on the basis of a shared language, and adapting the outcomes to the needs of all parties involved. Now all that remains is to move to the next phase: development that will enable children and adults alike who struggle with language learning to identify those difficulties accurately, overcome them, and save themselves a great deal of frustration in the language learning process.

As a linguistics graduate, it's hard for me to wait, and I highly recommend following Rami's work.
